Joseph Priestley (1765)

I want to begin by going back to the mid 18th century and the “time-as-an-arrow” linear metaphor.

This is part of Joseph Priestley's 'Chart of Biography', in which time is mapped from left to right; here he has drawn the lifespans of a number historical figures, offset vertically to avoid overdrawing. Otherwise there’s no meaning to the y axis aside from faceting the data with statesmen at the bottom and “men of learning” at the top.

This left-to-right design of time remains popular today, as are timelines drawn vertically.

© Webalon Ltd. (tiki-toki.com)

You may also encounter timelines like this one in which the viewer walks along the arrow of time in an egocentric way.

However, without the bird's eye perspective of the whole timeline, it can be hard to accurately gauge temporal distances between events.

Another common way that we represent time is the circle.
LadyofHats (Wikimedia Commons)
Radial representations are especially effective when representing natural cycles and events that repeat. Like biological life cycles or the seasons of the year.
flickr/46704958@N07
But perhaps you’re inclined to see time as both linear AND cyclic, as a coil or a spring, something that repeats and yet spirals upwards or forwards.
© 2015 Ross Institute & Moebio Labs (spiral.rosslearningsystem.org)

This spiral metaphor is particularly effective for timelines depicting growth, like a vine wrapping around a column.

Moebio Labs created this interactive spiral of timelines for the Ross Institute to emphasize the interconnectedness, repetition, and growth that occurs in a K-12 education curriculum.

flickr/studiocurve

Calendars are another familiar representation for time.

Like radial representations, calendars are good for showing repeating patterns and deviations from these patterns, particularly when these patterns correspond to culturally agreed-upon conventions of weekdays and weekends, and months.

Peabody’s Polish American System of Chronology (mid 19th century). Reference: Klein et al. (2016): shapeofhistory.net

In a way, the calendar is like a bookshelf, where each shelf might be a week with seven books it.

But the shelf-calendar metaphor works for larger granularities of time, like decades, centuries, and millennia.

One instantiation of this shelf metaphor is is Elizabeth Peabody's Polish American system of chronology from the mid-19th-century, which she proposed as an aid for teaching history, where each is century a 10 by 10 grid, each decade is a row and each cell a year, colored and patterned in according to the type of events that occurred in that year.

M. Twain (1914). "How to make history dates stick". Harper's Monthly Magazine.

And the last form of representation for time that I want to talk about is much less restrained by shape.

These playful curved timelines are by Mark Twain, which he proposed as a mnemonic aid for remembering dates using illustrative shapes.

© "The world’s greatest storyteller" (2014). Raconteur.net

A century later these curvy timelines are still popular, even if their shape doesn't serve as mnemonic. The curve may simply be the best way to fill a page.

What is a Timeline For?

The visual metaphor or representation of time is ONLY ONE aspect of the timeline design space. You also need to consider what a timeline is for, and how different combinations of REPRESENTATIONS and TIME SCALES tell different stories.

What happened when?

In what sequence did the events occur?

How long did the events last?

How long between event A and event B?

Did A and B co-occur?

When did A and B occur relative to event C?

Basically, a timeline tells a viewer “WHAT HAPPENED WHEN?”. But if we consider this question further, we arrive at more questions, like:

In what sequence did events occur? For how long did they occur? Did event A and event B co-occur? And when did they occur relative to some baseline event, say event C?

A Timeline Design Space

So, there are several design choices that go into telling stories with timelines.

There’s representation, as well as different scales like the absolute chronological scale, relative scales, log scales, and sequential scales.

And a third consideration which I’ll refer to as “Layout”, or how to draw timelines on a page: which has to do with deciding between a single timeline, faceting into multiple timelines, or wrapping a timeline into meaningful segments of time.

Common timeline designs at timelinesrevisited.github.io

This design space is based on survey of over 250 timelines and timeline tools that I did with my colleagues,

and in addition to identifying the dimensions of the design space, we identified 20 combinations of design choices that correspond to feasible perceptual tasks and meaningful narrative goals or intents,

as well as aesthetic tradeoffs and comments about aspect ratios.

And you can browse both the survey and galleries of examples at timelinesrevisited.github.io.

Storytelling With Timelines

So now that we’re equipped with design choices, I arrive at the question of how you can tell stories with timelines

how you can use and combine different points in this design space?

how can you leverage interactivity, animation, and annotation to tell these stories, to incrementally reveal parts of a narrative and make different types of comparisons, which you already saw in my golf example.

© (2010) Mnemograph LLC: timeglider.com
At one end of the storytelling spectrum, there's presenting an interactive choose-your-own adventure timeline, and there are quite a few tools out there that present timelines in this way, like this one called Timeglider. But these open-ended exploratory tools don’t guarantee that your story was communicated to the viewer; they are fine for open-ended data analysis, and there are certainly quite a few data analysis tools that look like this, but these tools don't guide the attention of the viewer along a narrative.
© (2013) Northwestern University Knight Lab: TimelineJS
At the other end of the storytelling spectrum, you can reveal a timeline as a slideshow in the linear, left-to-right chronological order in which the events occur, which is the approach taken by timeline presentation tools like Timeline.js, a popular presentation tool from the Northwestern University Knight Lab.

chronological Narrative:

Johnson was born,
THEN he started golfing,
THEN won the US Open, …

non-chronological Narrative:

Johnson won the US Open in 2016,
… THAT SAME YEAR, Furyk was runner-up, … ,
YEARS EARLIER, Furyk also won in his 9th US Open appearance, …

But neither of these approaches are how we tell stories.

I might start with the most important or relevant event, and then I add context: I flashback to earlier events, or add concurrent events happening in another timeline.

This was true in my U.S. open golf example, which didn't follow the chronological order of events

So if we are to tell compelling stories with timelines, our tools need to be more flexible and expressive.

For some stories, a chronological introduction of events makes sense, while for others it does not, like in my golf example.

And similarly, given the range of ways that we visually represent and scale time, our storytelling tools shouldn’t be constrained to linear representations and chronological time scales.

And so we built Timeline Storyteller, an open-source presentation tool that my colleagues and I initially released in January, which ran in the web browser. And as of three weeks ago, we shipped this as a free custom visualization component for Microsoft Power BI, which if you are unfamiliar is a business intelligence tool that's been out now for a couple of years, which you can get for free.

Timeline Storyteller is a scene-based presentation tool, where for in each scene in a story, the author can choose to hide, reveal, emphasize, an annotate part of their timeline, and choose a new representation or time scale to better suit their narrative intent.

Once you record a story, you can publish it to the web and embed it in a website or blog, as I have done in these slides.

Storytelling Considerations

Don't limit the story by using a single design space combination.

Use animated transitions and annotation for incremental reveal,
and to connect consecutive narrative points.

I want to finish by reiterating that despite the apparent simplicity of the question of "what happened when?", I've hopefully communicated the richness of the timeline design space in terms of different visual representations and time scales,

and this design space grows even richer when we introduce dynamic storytelling elements like incremental reveal, selective annotation and highlighting, and animated transitions between points in the design space.
