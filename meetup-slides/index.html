Expressive Storytelling with Timelines

Matthew Brehmer · Microsoft Research · @mattbrehmer
Seattle Uber Visualization Night · 08-31-2017 · slides: timelinesrevisited.github.io/meetup-slides

Thought history, people have been recording sequences of events so that they could tell stories about the past.

Today, I am going to talk about VISUAL Representations of these sequences of events, about TIMELINES and the different types of stories that they tell.

I’m going to show you a lot of design choices, give you a structured way to think about this design space, and then I’m going to introduce a new tool for creating expressive timeline stories..

Some visual representations of time:

Today I’m going to talk about some alternative representations for time, and hopefully give you a sense of when each is appropriate to use.

flickr/librarianishish

And I’ll begin with the most familiar, the “time-as-an-arrow” linear metaphor.

Joseph Priestley (1765)

This representation has dominated the designs of timelines for several centuries, made popular by Joseph Priestley in his 'Chart of Biography', in which time is mapped from left to right; here he has drawn the lifespans of a number historical figures, offset vertically to avoid overdrawing. Otherwise there’s no meaning to the y axis aside from faceting the data with statesmen at the bottom and “men of learning” at the top.

© Webalon Ltd. (tiki-toki.com)

These cardinal directions for time work great in print and in static images for giving the observer a bird’s eye or omniscient view of time.

But another common mental model for time is egocentric, one that places the observer along the line of time.

And that the arrow of time is like a museum hallway that you can walk down and examine its contents.

And we see this egocentric view of time in timelines like this one depicting the history of the Tower of London, which is a bit like standing on a moving sidewalk at an airport, with time flying past you.

flickr/shenghunglin
Another common way that we represent time is the circle, especially when representing natural cycles and events that repeat.
LadyofHats (Wikimedia Commons)
Like biological life cycles or the seasons of the year.
© Georgia Lupi (dear-data.com)

But what if your data doesn't contain cycles or periodic repetition?

Can you still use a circular or radial representation?

This is Giorgia Lupi's circular depiction of her life history as part of the Dear Data project, with her birth at 12 o’clock and her current age at 11 o’clock;

at which point the timeline veers upwards and trails off into uncertainty and NOT back towards her birth, indicating discontinuity and non-cyclicality.

flickr/46704958@N07
But perhaps you’re inclined to see time as both linear AND cyclic, as a coil or a spring, something that repeats and yet spirals upwards or forwards.
© 2015 Ross Institute & Moebio Labs (spiral.rosslearningsystem.org)

This spiral metaphor is particularly effective for timelines depicting growth, like a vine wrapping around a column.

Take for instance these spiralling timelines that Moebio Labs created for the Ross Institute to emphasize the interconnectedness, repetition, and growth that occurs in a K-12 education curriculum.

flickr/studiocurve

Calendars also encode different granularities of time in different ways, albeit in a nested structure of day cells within week rows within month polygons.

Like radial representations, calendars are good for showing repeating patterns and deviations from these patterns, particularly when these patterns correspond to culturally agreed-upon conventions of weekdays and weekends, months, and quarters.

Peabody’s Polish American System of Chronology (mid 19th century). Reference: Klein et al. (2016): shapeofhistory.net

In a way, the calendar is like a bookshelf, where each shelf might be a week with seven books it.

But the shelf-calendar metaphor works for larger granularities of time, like decades, centuries, and millennia.

One instantiation of this shelf metaphor is is Elizabeth Peabody's Polish American system of chronology from the mid-19th-century, which she proposed as an aid for teaching history, where each is century a 10 by 10 grid, each decade is a row and each cell a year, colored and patterned in according to the type of events that occurred in that year.

If you're interested in learning more about this approach, I suggest checking out an interactive revival of Peabody’s method by Lauren Klein and her colleagues in the digital humanities lab at Georgia Tech, at shapeofhistory.net

M. Twain (1914). "How to make history dates stick". Harper's Monthly Magazine.

And the last form of representation for time that I want to talk about is much less restrained by shape.

These playful curved timelines are by Mark Twain, which like Peabody’s system, he proposed as a mnemonic aid for remembering dates using illustrative shapes.

© "The world’s greatest storyteller" (2014). Raconteur.net

A century later these curvy timelines are still popular, even if their shape doesn't serve as mnemonic. The curve may simply be the best way to fill up a page.

What is a Timeline For?

The visual metaphor or representation of time is ONLY ONE aspect of the timeline design space. You also need to consider what a timeline is for, and how different combinations of REPRESENTATIONS and TIME SCALES tell different stories.
What happened when?
When and how much?
Where, what, and when?
What was related when?
What if and when?
Noun Project icons : @olsjoe, @mabusafiah, @makeiev, @barracuda, @ratch0013
So what is a timeline for? One way to go about answering this question is to try to distinguish the purpose of a timeline from other chart types that answer different questions involving time-oriented data.
What happened when?
When and how much?
Where, what, and when?
What was related when?
What if and when?
Noun Project icons : @olsjoe, @mabusafiah, @makeiev, @barracuda, @ratch0013

My simple answer is that a timeline serves to communicate WHAT happened WHEN.

Meanwhile, a line or area chart tells you HOW MUCH over time; a trajectory map combines WHERE, WHAT, and WHEN; dynamic graphs, or family trees will tell you about how entities were related over time, and flow charts answers the question of “WHAT IF?" over time, in which time can branch.

Of course, there are many instances in which a timeline is shown in conjunction with other chart types, or you may develop a custom chart that combines these in a novel way.

What happened when?

In what sequence did the events occur?

How long were the events?

Did events A and B co-occur?

When did events A and B occur relative to event C?

But if we focus on the deceptively simple question of “WHAT HAPPENED WHEN?” and try to take it apart, we arrive at more questions, like:

In what sequence did events occur? For how long did they occur? Did event A and event B co-occur? And when did they occur relative to a common baseline, say event C?

A Timeline Design Space

So, there are several design choices that go into telling stories with timelines.

There’s representation, as well as different scales like the absolute chronological scale, relative scales, log scales, and sequential scales.

And a third consideration which I’ll refer to as “Layout”, which has to do with deciding between a single timeline, faceting into multiple timelines, or wrapping a timeline into meaningful segments of time.

journal paper + survey + example galleries at timelinesrevisited.github.io

And in case you’re worried about the huge combinatorial space of design choices. Not to worry!

we wrote a journal paper about the timeline design space. We conducted a survey of over 250 timelines and timeline tools,

and then we identified 20 combinations of design choices that correspond to feasible perceptual tasks and meaningful narrative goals or intents,

as well as aesthetic tradeoffs and comments about aspect ratios.

And you can browse both the survey and galleries of examples at timelinesrevisited.github.io.

Storytelling With Timelines

So now that we’re equipped with design choices, I arrive at the question of storytelling and how you can tell stories with timelines in a dynamic medium;

how you can combine different points in this design space?

how can you leverage interactivity, animation, and annotation to tell these stories, to incrementally reveal parts of a narrative and make different types of comparisons, which you already saw a preview of in my Dali vs. Matisse story.

© (2010) Mnemograph LLC: timeglider.com
At one end of the storytelling spectrum, there's presenting an interactive choose-your-own adventure timeline, and there are quite a few tools out there that present timelines in this way, like this one called Timeglider. But these open-ended exploratory tools don’t guarantee that your story was communicated to the viewer; they are fine for open-ended data analysis, and there are certainly quite a few data analysis tools that look like this, but these tools don't guide the attention of the viewer along a narrative.
© (2013) Northwestern University Knight Lab: TimelineJS
At the other end of the storytelling spectrum, you can reveal a timeline as a slideshow in the linear, left-to-right chronological order in which the events occur, which is theapproach taken by timeline presentation tools like Timeline.js, a popular presentation tool from the Northwestern University Knight Lab.

chronological Narrative:

Johnson was born,
THEN he started golfing,
THEN won the US Open, …

non-chronological Narrative:

Johnson won the US Open in 2016,
… THAT SAME YEAR, Furyk was runner-up, … ,
YEARS EARLIER, Furyk also won in his 9th US Open appearance, …

So if we are to tell compelling stories with timelines, our tools need to be more flexible and expressive.

For some stories, a chronological introduction of events makes sense, while for others it does not, like in my earlier example of comparing the life of Dali with that of Matisse.

And similarly, given the range of ways that we visually represent and scale time, our storytelling tools shouldn’t be constrained to linear representations and chronological time scales.

Timeline Storyteller

Annotate, emphasize, reveal

Expressive storytelling with timelines is the goal of Timeline Storyteller, an open-source presentation tool that my colleagues and I released a few months ago, and I’ve used this tool to generate several of the examples that you’ve seen so far in this talk.

This is a scene-based presentation tool that runs in the web browser, where for in each scene in a story, the author can choose to hide, reveal, emphasize, an annotate part of their timeline, and choose a new representation or time scale to better suit their narrative intent.

I want to finish by reiterating that despite the apparent simplicity of the question of "what happened when?", I've hopefully communicated the richness of the timeline design space in terms of different visual representations and time scales,

and this design space grows even richer when we introduce dynamic storytelling elements like incremental reveal, selective annotation and highlighting, and animated transitions between points in the design space.
